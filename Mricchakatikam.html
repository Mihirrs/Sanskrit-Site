Q16

मृच्छकटिकस्य मूलस्त्रोतम् उद्दिश्य लिखत।

Write a note on the source materials of Mṛcchakaṭikam.

Answer

दरिद्रचारुदत्तम् → मृच्छकटिकम् (Bhāsa's Daridra-Cārudattam is the primary source)

Answer — English

Several Sanskrit works contain characters, events, and passages that resemble material in Mṛcchakaṭikam. The chief sources and parallels are:

1. Daridra-Cārudattam (Bhāsa) — Scholars widely accept this four-act play as the primary source. The first four acts of Mṛcchakaṭikam closely mirror its plot, characters, and even individual verses. The table below shows direct textual parallels.

Daridra-CārudattamMṛcchakaṭikam
श्रूणोमि गद्यं श्रवणाभ्याम्। अन्धकारपूरिताभ्यां नासापुटाभ्याम्॥श्रूणोमि माल्यगन्धम् अन्धकारपूरितया पुनर्नासिकया न सुव्यक्तं भूषणशब्दम्।
तव मम च दारुणा शोभा भविष्यति।मरणान्तिकं वैरं भविष्यति।
उत्कण्ठितस्य हृदयानुगता सखीव।उत्कण्ठितस्य हृदयानुगुणा वयस्या।

2. Abhijñānaśākuntalam (Kālidāsa) — The court scene in Mṛcchakaṭikam parallels the royal hall scene in Śākuntalam. Both heroines suffer because their beloved is distracted by royal duties.

3. Mudrarākṣasam (Viśākhadatta) — The trial scene and the condemned man's procession to the execution ground in Mṛcchakaṭikam resembles Act 5 of Mudrarākṣasam.

4. Daśakumāracaritam (Daṇḍin) — The love story of Rāgamañjarī and a Brāhmaṇa parallels the Vasantasenā-Cārudatta subplot. However, since Daṇḍin lived after Śūdraka, it is more likely that Daṇḍin borrowed from Śūdraka, not vice versa.

5. Kathāsaritsāgara (Somadeva) — Also contains a parallel love story (Rūpānikā and Lohajangha), but again the Mṛcchakaṭikam is older, so this is a case of borrowing from the play, not the reverse.

Q17

'मृच्छकटिक' इति नामस्य औचित्यं विवृणुत।

Explain the appropriateness of the title "Mṛcchakaṭikam".

Answer

Viśvanātha in the Sāhityadarpaṇa (6/148) says a play's title should be meaningful and suggestive of its content. Mṛcchakaṭikam is a prakaraṇa, and the usual convention for this form is to name the play after its hero and heroine. Śūdraka, however, departed from convention and named it after a key incident in Act Six instead.

The episode behind the title: Cārudatta's young son Rohasena weeps because he wants to play with a golden toy-cart like the ones he has seen at a neighbour's house, but his father is too poor to afford one. Vasantasenā, learning of this, fills the toy-cart with her own jewels so that a gold one may be made for him. This moving act of generosity — the exchange of a clay cart (mṛt-śakaṭikā) for a golden one — gives the play its name: Mṛcchakaṭikam.

The title thus captures both the humble material circumstances of the hero and the nobility of spirit that transcends them — making it thematically apt even while unconventional.

Q18

मृच्छकटिकस्य चतुर्थाङ्कस्य मुख्यविषयः कः?

What is the main subject of Act Four of Mṛcchakaṭikam?

Answer

Act Four is one of the most important acts of the play. Its central themes are:

1. Vasantasenā's pure love for Cārudatta — Though she is a wealthy courtesan (gaṇikā), Vasantasenā's love for the impoverished Cārudatta is wholly selfless. She is drawn not by his wealth (which he no longer has) but entirely by his noble character — his generosity, kindness, humility, and concern for others.

2. The villainy of Śakāra — Śakāra's cruel desire to possess Vasantasenā by force is laid bare in this act. She rejects him utterly. This rejection sets in motion the central conflict of the play.

3. Contrast of virtue and vice — The act vividly juxtaposes Cārudatta's nobility with Śakāra's baseness, and Vasantasenā's dignified love with Śakāra's predatory lust.

In sum, Act Four establishes the moral centre of the drama — love, inner conflict, and the opposition between virtue and corruption.

Q19

मैत्रेयस्य पात्रस्य वैशिष्ट्यं वर्णयत।

Describe the character of Maitreya.

Answer

Maitreya fulfils the role of the vidūṣaka (comic companion) in Mṛcchakaṭikam and is Cārudatta's closest friend. His character has several notable features:

Comic and candid — His speech and behaviour create humour throughout the play. He is blunt, says what he thinks, and deflates pretension with casual directness.

Loyal and courageous — Despite his comic persona, Maitreya is deeply devoted to Cārudatta. He shares in his joys and sorrows, and fearlessly stands up to Śakāra's harassment when it threatens those he cares for.

Intelligent and dutiful — He is trusted with Vasantasenā's jewels (in Act 1) and carries out his responsibilities faithfully.

Ideal of friendship — Śūdraka uses Maitreya not merely for comic relief but as a portrait of true friendship (maitri) — selfless, constant, and courageous. He is a model companion, not just a jester.

Q20

मदनिकायाः पात्रचित्रणम्।

Describe the character of Madanikā.

Answer

Madanikā is one of the significant characters of Mṛcchakaṭikam and serves as Vasantasenā's personal attendant. Her portrayal goes well beyond that of a mere servant:

Intelligent and charming — She is perceptive, sweet-spoken, and knows how to conduct herself in every situation. She handles complex situations with finesse.

Loyal and trusted — Vasantasenā's affection for Madanikā is genuine and deep. She is not just a maidservant but Vasantasenā's most trusted confidante and inner companion.

Emblem of ideal womanhood — Though she lives in a courtesan's household, Madanikā is portrayed as an ideal woman of good counsel and noble sentiment.

Love story with Śarvilaka — Śarvilaka loves her and eventually frees her from bondage (dāsya). This love story forms an important subplot, and Madanikā's liberation — her happy ending — is Śūdraka's affirmation that virtue, even in difficult circumstances, finds its reward.

Her dialogues consistently add beauty and depth to the play.

Q21

शूद्रकस्य जीवनं, कृतिपरिचयं च कुरुत।

Give an account of Śūdraka's life and works.

Answer

Introduction
Mṛcchakaṭikam holds an honoured place in Sanskrit dramatic literature, yet its author Śūdraka remains a figure of considerable mystery. Almost all we know of him comes from the preamble of the play itself.

Description in the Preamble
The prologue describes Śūdraka as: powerful, prosperous, learned in the Ṛgveda, Sāmaveda, mathematics, the sixty-four arts, commerce, and elephant-training; a devotee of Śiva who, by Śiva's grace, had his eyes (previously blind with ignorance) opened. After performing the Aśvamedha sacrifice, handing the kingdom to his son, and living to 110 years and ten days, he entered fire (agnipraviṣṭa). Three famous verses from this preamble are:

द्विरदेन्द्रगतिश्चकोरनेत्रः परिपूर्णेन्दुमुखः सुविग्रहश्च।
द्विजमुख्यतमः कविर्बभूव प्रथितः शूद्रकेत्यगाद्यसत्त्वः॥

ऋग्वेदं सामवेदं गणितमथ कलां वैशिकीं हस्तिशिक्षां
ज्ञात्वा शर्वप्रसादात् व्यपगततिमिरो चक्षुषी चोपलभ्य।
राजानं वीक्ष्य पुत्रं परमसमुदयेनाश्वमेधेन चेष्ट्वा
लब्ध्वा चायुः शताब्दं दशदिनसहितं शूद्रकोऽग्निं प्रविष्टः॥

The puzzling fact: how could the author describe his own death in the preamble? This has led some scholars to suggest the preamble was written by a later hand, or that "Śūdraka" is a pen-name.

Historical References to Śūdraka
Several other works mention the name Śūdraka. The Skanda Purāṇa's Kumārikā Khaṇḍa mentions a heroic king named Śūdraka who would arise in the Kali age. Historians such as Dr. Smith have identified this figure with Simuka (or Śiśuka), the first king of the Āndhra dynasty. Prof. Stein Konow proposed that Śūdraka was another name for Śivadatta, a king of the Ābhīra dynasty. Rājaśekhara (in Kāvyamīmāṃsā) mentions two poets — Rābhila and Sobhila — who composed a work called "Śūdraka-kathā." Bāṇabhaṭṭa (Harshacarita, Kādambarī) refers to a king of Vidiśā named Śūdraka. Daṇḍin (Daśakumāracarita), Vetālapañcaviṃśati, and Kathāsaritsāgara also contain references to the name. Despite this wealth of references, there is no scholarly consensus on which "Śūdraka" composed the play.

Personality of Śūdraka
From the play's preamble: Śūdraka was a Brāhmaṇa (dvija) of heroic and contemplative temperament — warrior, scholar, and ascetic in one. He was well-versed in Vedic lore, mathematics, fine arts, elephant-lore, commerce, and also (notably) in the arts of theft (caurakala) and gambling (dyūtakala), which suggests broad familiarity with real social life. His mastery of both Sanskrit and Prākrit is evident throughout the play.

Region
Linguistic evidence in the play points to a southern origin. The word khuṇḍamoṭaka (Act 2), the epithet sahyavāsinī for the goddess (Act 10), the term nāṇaka for money (Act 1), and the expression karnāṭa-kalaha (Act 6) all suggest the author was from the Deccan / South India.

Date
There is much scholarly debate. Some place Śūdraka before the common era; others as late as 6th century CE. Based on internal and external evidence, a date broadly between 2nd century BCE and 6th century CE is generally accepted, with most placing him in the early centuries CE.

Works
Mṛcchakaṭikam is his undisputed masterwork. Daṇḍin and Vāmana mention other compositions by Śūdraka, but none have survived. A play called Padya-prābhṛtaka (a bhāṇa) circulates in South India under his name, but its authenticity remains uncertain.

Conclusion
Śūdraka remains one of Sanskrit literature's most intriguing figures — a genius who left one extraordinary play and almost no biography. Yet from that single work, a vivid personality emerges: humane, witty, socially perceptive, and deeply compassionate.

Q22

मृच्छकटिकस्य प्रथमाङ्कस्य सारांशं लिखत।

Write a summary of the First Act of Mṛcchakaṭikam.

Answer

Introduction
The first act of Mṛcchakaṭikam introduces all the main characters and sets the central plot in motion. It opens with the traditional nāndī (benedictory verse) and the sūtradhāra's (stage manager's) prologue.

Prologue (Prastāvanā)
The sūtradhāra, tired from music rehearsal, prepares to go home for a meal. He finds the house unusually decorated and asks his wife why. She explains she had taken a vow called "abhirūpapati-vrata" (vow for a handsome husband) which is now complete, and she needs to feed a Brāhmaṇa to fulfil it. The sūtradhāra goes in search of a Brāhmaṇa and encounters Maitreya, Cārudatta's friend, but despite offers of fine food and a fee (dakṣiṇā), Maitreya refuses to go. The sūtradhāra departs, and the prologue ends.

Entry of Vidūṣaka (Maitreya)
Maitreya enters the stage and presents Cārudatta with a fragrant upper garment (uttarīya), sent by their mutual friend Cūrṇadatta. Cārudatta, receiving it, muses sadly on how poverty changes people's behaviour toward you — a man once respected for his wealth is ignored when he is poor.

Cārudatta and the Domestic Ritual
Cārudatta asks Maitreya to go and offer the bali (ritual offering) to the household deities. Maitreya protests — "What's the point of all this worship?" Cārudatta answers him and then goes himself to perform the evening prayers (sandhyāvandana).

Vasantasenā's Arrival
Meanwhile, on the royal road, Śakāra (the king's wicked brother-in-law), his servant (ceṭa), and the parasite Viṭa are pursuing Vasantasenā. Fleeing in the darkness, she arrives at Cārudatta's door and seeks shelter.

The Night Scene
Cārudatta and Maitreya return to the stage. Maitreya takes a lamp and goes inside with the maid Radanikā to offer the bali. Vasantasenā, already inside, extinguishes the lamp to hide. Maitreya sends Radanikā to wait at the door and goes back in to relight the lamp. At that moment, Śakāra arrives, mistakes Radanikā for Vasantasenā and tries to embrace her. Maitreya, furious at this affront, threatens to behead him. The parasite Viṭa calms the situation. Despite urging, Śakāra does not leave immediately — he threatens Cārudatta before finally departing. Maitreya and Radanikā re-enter, and Maitreya tells Cārudatta all that happened (except the specific insult to Radanikā).

The Meeting of Cārudatta and Vasantasenā
Cārudatta, in the dim light, mistakes Vasantasenā for the maid Radanikā and asks her to take his son Rohasena inside. She hesitates, overcome with shyness. When Cārudatta and Vasantasenā finally recognise each other, a mutual awareness and admiration is established. She leaves her jewels (ābharaṇas) with Cārudatta for safekeeping, entrusting them to Vardhamānaka by day and to Maitreya by night. Cārudatta and Maitreya then escort Vasantasenā home, and the first act concludes.

Significance of Act One
Act One introduces: (a) the contrast between Cārudatta's noble poverty and Śakāra's vicious wealth; (b) the beginning of the love between Cārudatta and Vasantasenā; (c) the device of the jewels, which will drive the plot forward; and (d) the humour and loyalty of Maitreya.
