Q1

कति वैदिकदर्शनानि सन्ति?

How many vaidika darśanas are there?

Answer

षट् (Six) — न्यायः, वैशेषिकः, साङ्ख्यः, योगः, मीमांसा, वेदान्तश्च

Six (ṣaṭ-darśanas) — Nyāya, Vaiśeṣika, Sāṅkhya, Yoga, Mīmāṃsā, and Vedānta

Q2

सांख्यदर्शनानुसारं चैतन्यः नाम कः?

According to Sāṅkhya philosophy, what is caitanya?

Answer

पुरुषः (Puruṣa) — चैतन्यं पुरुषस्य स्वरूपम्

Caitanya (consciousness) is Puruṣa itself — consciousness is the very nature (svarūpa) of Puruṣa, not a quality of matter

Q3

कां कारिकां पुरातनीं कारिकाम् इति मन्यन्ते?

Which kārikā is considered the oldest kārikā?

Answer

ईश्वरकृष्णविरचिता साङ्ख्यकारिका (The Sāṅkhyakārikā of Īśvarakṛṣṇa)

The Sāṅkhyakārikā composed by Īśvarakṛṣṇa — the oldest available authoritative text of the Sāṅkhya system

Q4

"सांख्य" इत्यस्य अर्थं लिखत।

Write the meaning of "Sāṅkhya".

Answer

संख्या नाम सम्यग्ज्ञानं तत्त्वगणना च (Saṅkhyā means right knowledge and enumeration of tattvas)

"Sāṅkhya" is derived from saṅkhyā, meaning both right discriminative knowledge (samyag-jñāna) and enumeration — the system that enumerates the twenty-five tattvas

Q5

पञ्चकर्मेन्द्रियाणि कानि?

Which are the pañca-karmendriyas?

Answer

वाक्, पाणिः, पादः, पायुः, उपस्थश्च

Speech (vāk), hands (pāṇi), feet (pāda), organ of excretion (pāyu), and organ of generation (upastha)

Q6

सांख्यदर्शनानुसारं कति तत्त्वानि सन्ति?

According to Sāṅkhya philosophy, how many tattvas are there?

Answer

पञ्चविंशतिः (Twenty-five)

Twenty-five tattvas — Puruṣa, Prakṛti, and the twenty-three evolutes of Prakṛti

Q7

Sage Kapila — the founder of the Sāṅkhya system

Q8

साङ्ख्यमतानुसारम् अव्यक्तं नाम किम्?

According to Sāṅkhya, what is avyakta?

Answer

मूलप्रकृतिः / प्रधानम् (Mūla-prakṛti, also called Pradhāna)

Avyakta (the unmanifest) is mūla-prakṛti — the primordial, uncaused root-matter in its unmanifest state of equilibrium of the three guṇas

Q10

सांख्यदर्शने कति प्रमाणानि सन्ति?

How many pramāṇas are there in Sāṅkhya philosophy?

Answer

त्रीणि (Three) — प्रत्यक्षम्, अनुमानम्, आप्तवचनं (शब्दः) च

Three — perception (pratyakṣa/dṛṣṭa), inference (anumāna), and reliable testimony (āptavacana/śabda)

Answer in Brief (Any Four)

Q11

सांख्यदर्शनानुसारं कति प्रमाणानि सन्ति? तानि व्याख्यात।

According to Sāṅkhya philosophy, how many pramāṇas are there? Explain them.

Answer

दृष्टमनुमानमाप्तवचनं च सर्वप्रमाणसिद्धत्वात्।
त्रिविधं प्रमाणमिष्टं प्रमेयसिद्धिः प्रमाणाद्धि॥ (साङ्ख्यकारिका ४)

Answer — English

Sāṅkhya accepts exactly three pramāṇas (means of valid knowledge), since all others reduce to these (Kārikā 4). 1. Dṛṣṭa / Pratyakṣa (Perception) — direct cognition from sense-object contact; defined as the determinate ascertainment of each sense with its own object (prativiṣayādhyavasāya). 2. Anumāna (Inference) — knowledge of an unperceived object through the invariable relation (vyāpti) between a mark and what it marks; of three kinds (pūrvavat, śeṣavat, sāmānyatodṛṣṭa); used chiefly to establish Prakṛti and Puruṣa. 3. Āptavacana / Śabda (Reliable Testimony) — the statement of a trustworthy authority including the Veda, yielding knowledge beyond the reach of perception and inference (e.g., heaven, subtle dharma). By perception we know the gross world, by inference the subtle principles, and by testimony the supersensuous truths — together they establish the entire scheme of twenty-five tattvas.

Q12

सांख्यदर्शने गुणत्रयाणां भूमिकां निरूपयत।

Evaluate the role of the three guṇas in the Sāṅkhya system.

Answer

सत्त्वं लघु प्रकाशकमिष्टमुपष्टम्भकं चलं च रजः।
गुरु वरणकमेव तमः प्रदीपवच्चार्थतो वृत्तिः॥ (साङ्ख्यकारिका १३)

Answer — English

The three guṇas — sattva, rajas, and tamas — are not mere qualities but the very constituents (strands) of Prakṛti, lying in perfect equilibrium (sāmyāvasthā) in the unmanifest state. When Puruṣa's proximity disturbs this equilibrium, evolution begins and all subsequent tattvas emerge; every change in the universe is their interplay. Sattva is light (laghu) and illuminating (prakāśaka), producing pleasure, clarity, and knowledge. Rajas is mobile (cala) and stimulating (upaṣṭambhaka), producing pain, passion, and activity; it alone moves the other two. Tamas is heavy (guru) and enveloping (varaṇaka), producing inertia, sloth, and ignorance. Though mutually opposed, they always cooperate for a single purpose — like the wick, oil, and flame of a lamp (pradīpavat). Bondage is the unchecked play of the guṇas; liberation is Puruṣa's discrimination from them.

Q13

महत् नाम किम्? व्याख्यात।

What is Mahat? Explain.

Answer

अध्यवसायो बुद्धिः धर्मो ज्ञानं विराग ऐश्वर्यम्।
सात्त्विकमेतद्रूपं तामसमस्माद्विपर्यस्तम्॥ (साङ्ख्यकारिका २३)

Answer — English

Mahat ("the Great One") is the first evolute (vikṛti) of Prakṛti, arising when the guṇa-equilibrium is disturbed. In its individual aspect it is called Buddhi (intellect), whose defining function is adhyavasāya — determination and definite ascertainment ("this is a pot," "this is to be done"). Cosmically, Mahat is the great germ of the universe; individually, it is the highest instrument of the inner organ (antaḥkaraṇa). When sattva predominates, Buddhi possesses dharma, jñāna, virāga, and aiśvarya; when tamas predominates, their four opposites appear (Kārikā 23). As both an effect of Prakṛti and a cause of Ahaṅkāra, it is a prakṛti-vikṛti. Standing nearest to Puruṣa, it is in Buddhi that Puruṣa's reflection appears, and it is Buddhi's discriminative knowledge (viveka-khyāti) that ultimately brings liberation.

Q14

सांख्यदर्शने दुःखत्रयं किम्? संक्षेपेण व्याख्यात।

What is duḥkhatraya (trividha-duḥkha) as per Sāṅkhya philosophy? Explain briefly.

Answer

दुःखत्रयाभिघाताज्जिज्ञासा तदपघातके हेतौ।
दृष्टे सापार्था चेन्नैकान्तात्यन्ततोऽभावात्॥ (साङ्ख्यकारिका १)

Answer — English

The opening kārikā states that the torment of threefold suffering (duḥkhatraya) generates the desire (jijñāsā) to know the means of its removal — the starting point of the whole Sāṅkhya inquiry. 1. Ādhyātmika — suffering from one's own body and mind: bodily pain (fever, disease) and mental pain (desire, anger, grief, fear). 2. Ādhibhautika — suffering caused by other creatures: men, beasts, birds, serpents, insects, and so on. 3. Ādhidaivika — suffering from cosmic and supernatural forces: planetary influences, heat, cold, storm, flood, and spirits. Ordinary (dṛṣṭa) remedies like medicine and Vedic (ānuśravika) remedies like sacrifice cannot remove suffering absolutely (ekānta-atyanta-abhāva). Only the discriminative knowledge of Vyakta, Avyakta, and Jña — the manifest, the unmanifest, and the knowing subject — brings complete and final cessation: mokṣa (apavarga).

Q15

सांख्यदर्शनानुसारं सत्कार्यवादं विशदयत।

Define and explain Satkāryavāda according to Sāṅkhyadarśana.

Answer

असदकरणादुपादानग्रहणात् सर्वसम्भवाभावात्।
शक्तस्य शक्यकरणात् कारणभावाच्च सत्कार्यम्॥ (साङ्ख्यकारिका ९)

Answer — English

Satkāryavāda is the Sāṅkhya theory of causation holding that the effect (kārya) pre-exists (sat) in its material cause before production; production is merely manifestation (āvirbhāva) of the latent, and destruction is its concealment (tirobhāva). Kārikā 9 offers five arguments: (1) Asad-akaraṇāt — the non-existent cannot be produced (no one makes oil from sand); (2) Upādāna-grahaṇāt — a specific material cause is always chosen (curd from milk, not water), proving the effect is latent there; (3) Sarva-sambhava-abhāvāt — if effects were not pre-existent in causes, anything could arise from anything; (4) Śaktasya śakya-karaṇāt — a cause produces only what it has the potency (śakti) for; (5) Kāraṇa-bhāvāt — effect and cause are non-different in essence (cloth is not a different substance from its threads). On this basis Sāṅkhya infers the unmanifest Prakṛti as the world's ultimate material cause. Since the transformation is real, this view is called pariṇāmavāda, distinct from Advaita's vivartavāda (merely apparent change).

Q16

त्रिगुणानां नामानि लिखत? संक्षेपेण व्याख्यात।

Write the names of the triguṇas and explain briefly.

Answer

सत्त्वं, रजः, तमश्च इति त्रयो गुणाः। प्रीत्यप्रीतिविषादात्मकाः प्रकाशप्रवृत्तिनियमार्थाः।

Answer — English

The three guṇas — Sattva, Rajas, and Tamas — are the fundamental constituents of Prakṛti, respectively of the nature of pleasure, pain, and delusion (prīti-aprīti-viṣāda-ātmaka), serving the purposes of illumination, activity, and restraint. Sattva is light (laghu) and illuminating (prakāśaka): it manifests as clarity, knowledge, purity, and pleasure. Rajas is mobile (cala) and stimulating (upaṣṭambhaka): the principle of energy, manifesting as pain, passion, and effort; without rajas the other two cannot function. Tamas is heavy (guru) and enveloping (varaṇaka): the principle of inertia, manifesting as sloth, sleep, and ignorance. Though mutually opposed, the guṇas always cooperate for a single purpose — like the wick, oil, and flame of a lamp (pradīpavat). In Prakṛti they lie in equilibrium; the disturbance of that equilibrium initiates the evolution of the world.

Q17

सांख्यस्य द्वैतवादि-तत्त्वशास्त्रस्य चर्चां कुरुत। पुरुषः प्रकृतिश्च परस्परं कथं वर्तेते?

Discuss the dualistic metaphysics of Sāṅkhya. How do Puruṣa and Prakṛti interact?

Answer

Introduction
Sāṅkhya is the classic dualistic (dvaitavādi) system of Indian philosophy, explaining all experience through two ultimate, eternal, independent realities: Puruṣa (pure consciousness) and Prakṛti (unconscious primordial matter). Everything is either one of these or an evolute of Prakṛti witnessed by Puruṣa.

Prakṛti — the Unconscious Root-Matter
Prakṛti (Pradhāna/Avyakta) is the uncaused, eternal, all-pervading material cause of the world — one, active, unconscious (acetana), and constituted of the three guṇas in equilibrium. Its existence is proved by inference (Kārikā 15): finite, dependent effects require an unmanifest ultimate cause. Kārikā 3 classifies: mūla-prakṛti is uncaused; Mahat, Ahaṅkāra, and the five tanmātras are both cause and effect; the sixteen (manas, ten indriyas, five mahābhūtas) are effects only; Puruṣa is neither.

मूलप्रकृतिरविकृतिर्महदाद्याः प्रकृतिविकृतयः सप्त।
षोडशकस्तु विकारो न प्रकृतिर्न विकृतिः पुरुषः॥ (साङ्ख्यकारिका ३)

Puruṣa — Pure Consciousness
Puruṣa is pure contentless consciousness (cit): eternal witness (sākṣī), inactive (akartā), beyond the guṇas, and ever-free (kevalī), as Kārikā 19 declares. Its existence is argued in Kārikā 17: composite objects exist for the sake of another (saṅghāta-parārthatvāt); there must be an experiencer (bhoktā) distinct from the guṇas; liberation presupposes a spirit capable of being freed. Sāṅkhya further establishes a plurality of Puruṣas (Kārikā 18): birth, death, and faculties are allotted differently to different individuals. Prakṛti is object, unconscious, active, one, and ever-changing; Puruṣa is subject, conscious, inactive, many, and changeless — hence the strict dualism.

How Puruṣa and Prakṛti Interact
Since Puruṣa is inactive and Prakṛti is blind, neither alone produces the world. Their association (saṃyoga) is illustrated by the lame-and-blind analogy (Kārikā 21): the lame man (who sees but cannot walk) mounts the blind man (who walks but cannot see) and both reach their goal.
पुरुषस्य दर्शनार्थं कैवल्यार्थं तथा प्रधानस्य।
पङ्ग्वन्धवदुभयोरपि संयोगस्तत्कृतः सर्गः॥ (साङ्ख्यकारिका २१)

The union serves two ends: Puruṣa gains experience (bhoga), and Prakṛti works toward his eventual isolation (kaivalya). The mere proximity (sannidhi) of Puruṣa disturbs the guṇa-equilibrium, starting evolution; Prakṛti acts unconsciously for Puruṣa's ends, as milk flows for the calf (Kārikā 57).

Bondage and Liberation
Through non-discrimination (aviveka), Puruṣa wrongly identifies with buddhi and the body; in truth it is Prakṛti that binds and releases herself — "no Puruṣa is really bound or released" (Kārikā 62). Like a dancer who retires after showing herself (Kārikā 59), Prakṛti withdraws once truly seen. When viveka-khyāti — "I am not this, nothing is mine, there is no ego" — arises, the guṇas cease their work and Puruṣa abides in its own nature: kaivalya.

Conclusion
Sāṅkhya's dualism accounts for the objective world through Prakṛti's evolution and for liberation through Puruṣa's discrimination. The teleological cooperation of unconscious matter serving spirit is its most distinctive feature, though critics (notably Vedāntins) question how an inactive Puruṣa and a blind Prakṛti can truly enter into relation.

Q18

साङ्ख्ये चतुर्विंशतितत्त्वानां प्रक्रियां महत्त्वं च वर्णयत।

Describe the process and significance of the 24 Tattvas in Sāṅkhya.

Answer

Introduction
Sāṅkhya enumerates 25 tattvas: Puruṣa (pure consciousness) and 24 material principles — Prakṛti plus its 23 evolutes. The order of evolution is stated in Kārikā 22:

प्रकृतेर्महांस्ततोऽहङ्कारस्तस्माद्गणश्च षोडशकः।
तस्मादपि षोडशकात् पञ्चभ्यः पञ्च भूतानि॥ (साङ्ख्यकारिका २२)

The Process of Evolution (Sṛṣṭi-krama)
1. Mūla-Prakṛti (Avyakta) — The uncaused root; the three guṇas in equilibrium. Puruṣa's proximity disturbs this, starting evolution.
2. Mahat (Buddhi) — First evolute; the principle of determination (adhyavasāya). Cosmically the world's germ; individually the intellect.
3. Ahaṅkāra — Ego-principle (abhimāna: "I" and "mine"). From its sāttvika aspect (vaikṛta) arise the eleven (manas + ten indriyas); from its tāmasa aspect (bhūtādi) the five tanmātras; the rājasa aspect (taijasa) energises both.
4. Manas (Mind) — Synthesises sense-data (saṅkalpaka); acts as both cognitive and conative organ.
5–9. Pañca-jñānendriyas — Five organs of knowledge: eye (cakṣus), ear (śrotra), nose (ghrāṇa), tongue (rasanā), skin (tvak).
10–14. Pañca-karmendriyas — Five organs of action: speech (vāk), hands (pāṇi), feet (pāda), excretion (pāyu), generation (upastha).
15–19. Pañca-tanmātras — Five subtle essences: sound, touch, form, taste, smell — imperceptible to ordinary beings.
20–24. Pañca-mahābhūtas — Five gross elements from the tanmātras in order: ākāśa, vāyu, tejas, ap, pṛthivī — each possessing the qualities of the preceding.

Classification (Kārikā 3)
(a) Mūla-prakṛti — cause only; (b) Mahat, Ahaṅkāra, five tanmātras (seven) — both cause and effect; (c) Manas, ten indriyas, five mahābhūtas (sixteen) — effects only; (d) Puruṣa — neither. Buddhi, ahaṅkāra, and manas form the antaḥkaraṇa; together with the ten senses they comprise the trayodaśa-karaṇa (thirteen-fold instrument) of experience.

Significance
(i) The scheme is a complete map of experience: mind and matter are shown as two lines of development from one Prakṛti. (ii) It demonstrates satkāryavāda: each evolute pre-exists in its cause; dissolution is the reverse reabsorption (pralaya). (iii) The whole evolution is teleological — serving Puruṣa's bhoga (experience) and apavarga (liberation), as milk flows for the calf. (iv) Discrimination of the 25th principle (Puruṣa) from the 24 is itself liberating knowledge — the tattva-scheme is the ladder of viveka. The framework profoundly influenced Yoga, Āyurveda, the Purāṇas, and the Bhagavadgītā.

Conclusion
The tattva doctrine is Sāṅkhya's answer to "What is this world and what am I in it?" By tracing all objects to Prakṛti and reserving consciousness for Puruṣa alone, it converts metaphysics into a path of discrimination leading to kaivalya.

Q19

साङ्ख्यानुसारं कारणत्वसिद्धान्तं (सत्कार्यवादः) समीक्षात्मकरूपेण व्याख्यात। सत्कार्यवादः असत्कार्यवादात् कथं भिद्यते?

Critically explain the Sāṅkhya theory of causation (Satkāryavāda). How does it differ from Asatkāryavāda?

Answer

Introduction
The problem of causation divides Indian philosophy. Sāṅkhya upholds Satkāryavāda: the effect (kārya) pre-exists (sat) in its material cause before production. Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika upholds Asatkāryavāda (ārambhavāda): the effect is non-existent before production — a wholly fresh beginning. Sāṅkhya's inference of Prakṛti itself rests on satkāryavāda, established in Kārikā 9:

असदकरणादुपादानग्रहणात् सर्वसम्भवाभावात्।
शक्तस्य शक्यकरणात् कारणभावाच्च सत्कार्यम्॥ (साङ्ख्यकारिका ९)

Five Arguments for Satkāryavāda
1. Asad-akaraṇāt — The non-existent cannot be produced; no one makes oil from sand or conjures a hare's horn into being.
2. Upādāna-grahaṇāt — A specific material cause is invariably chosen: curd from milk, not water — the fixed relation shows the effect is latent in that particular material.
3. Sarva-sambhava-abhāvāt — If the effect were non-existent in the cause, anything could arise from anything; the restriction to specific causes proves pre-existence.
4. Śaktasya śakya-karaṇāt — A cause produces only what it has the potency (śakti) for; that potency is the effect in its latent state.
5. Kāraṇa-bhāvāt — Effect and cause are non-different in essence: cloth is not a different substance from its threads.

Production is therefore manifestation (āvirbhāva) of the latent; destruction is concealment (tirobhāva). Since the change is a real transformation, this is called pariṇāmavāda, distinct from Advaita's vivartavāda (merely apparent change).

Asatkāryavāda — the Opposed View
Nyāya-Vaiśeṣika holds that the effect is a genuinely new entity, non-existent before production. Their arguments: (a) if the effect existed already, production and the craftsman's effort would be pointless; (b) cause and effect are spoken of differently, so they must be distinct; (c) the effect serves purposes the cause cannot (a pot carries water; clay does not).

How the Two Views Differ
For Sāṅkhya, production is manifestation of the latent; for Nyāya, fresh creation. For Sāṅkhya, cause and effect are essentially identical — one substance in different states (avasthā-bheda); for Nyāya, distinct entities related by inherence (samavāya). Satkāryavāda leads to one continuous substratum — Prakṛti; asatkāryavāda supports atomistic pluralism (paramāṇuvāda).

Critical Estimate
Sāṅkhya replies that effort is still needed to manifest the latent (oil pre-exists in the seed, yet pressing is required), and difference of purpose reflects difference of state, not substance. Critics press: if the effect is wholly existent, "production" loses meaning; the Advaitin adds that real transformation of an eternal Prakṛti is incoherent. Nevertheless, satkāryavāda preserves the continuity of nature ("nothing from nothing"), anticipates conservation of matter and energy, and provides the logical ground for inferring the unmanifest Prakṛti as the world's root cause.

Conclusion
Satkāryavāda is the keystone of Sāṅkhya: upon the pre-existence of the effect rest its doctrines of Prakṛti, the tattva-order, and dissolution. It remains one of the most carefully argued theories of causation in Indian philosophy.
